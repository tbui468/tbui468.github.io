1 INTRODUCTION

Apples are one of the most important fruit crops in the world. As the world population grows and food demands increase, the cultivation of apple will continue to be an essential part of the human food supply. One major concern for apple production is foliar diseases. Most current methods of apple disease detection rely on human experts. The use of human experts for disease detection is time consuming and expensive. Automatic disease detection using artificial neural networks is one possible solution to this problem. In addition to the costs savings from not requiring opinions of an expert, using an artificial neural network allows detection of diseases in a quick, and accurate manner.

2 RELATED WORK

Computer vision using articifial neural networks trained by gradient descent revolutionized image recognition first by Yan LeCun et al. (1989) and again by Krizhevsky et al. (2012). Since then the use of convolutional networks has become the mainstay in many computer vision tasks.

The Transformer has revolutionized natural language processing (NLP) since being introduced in 2017. Using 'attention', transformers processed inputs concurrently as opposed to sequentially at each time-step. Along with speeding up the training process, the use of attention allowed the network to keep track of longer term dependencies than RNNs. The use of transformers in computer vision is still in its infancy. Early results from Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) suggest that transformers perform just as well as convolutional networks on different vision-based tasks, and larger transformers have the potential to surpass convolutional networks.

3 METHODS

3.1 DATA

The entire dataset consists of 23,000 images and labels. The training data provided consists of 18,632 images and their corresponding labels, and the remaining is set aside as the test data. The image is labeled by experts with up to the 6 different labels: healthy, frog eye leaf spot, powdery mildew, rust, scab, and complex. The label complex is applied to any specimen with too many diseases to easily classify. In the case that an image is labeled 'complex', a subset of the diseases may also be labeled. Table 1 shows the distribution of the labels. The 'healthy' label is mutually exclusive, and will only appear if no diseases are detected. We've decided to allow the network to learn this rather than manually applying the 'healthy' label to images with no detected diseases.

All the jpeg images contain 3 color channels (rgb), and have widths between 2592 and 5184 pixels, and heights between 1728 and 4032 pixels. Reading images multiple large images from disk becomes the main bottleneck during training, therefore all 18632 images were converted to pytorch tensors and saved as a single file on disk. The images are first scaled down so that the smallest spacial dimension is equal to 384, and then centercropped to give a final size of 384x384. The labels are also processed and saved as pytorch tensors.

3.3 MODEL

The ViT weights are publicly available for all three ViT sizes: Base, Large and Huge. All models were pre-trained on ImageNet21k, with variants also further fine-tuned on ImageNet2012. Figure 3 shows the architecture of ViT. The authors of ViT pre-trained the network at a resolution of 224x224, and then fine-tuned at a higher resolution of 384x384. Experimental evidence suggests that fine-tuning at a higher resolution than the pre-training resolution results in improved performance. We haved followed this procedure and fine-tuned the model on images with an input resolution of 384x384. The results of the ViT will be compared to Densenet169 and EfficientNEt B7.

Figure 3: Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture. The image was taken directly from "An Image is Worth 16x16 Words: Transformers for Image Recognition at Scale".


4 EXPERIMENT

4.1 SETUP

We used the ViT Base model that was pre-trained on ImageNet21k at 224x224, and then fine-tuned on ImageNet2012 at 384x384. Training was done using Google Cloud Compute, using a NVIDIA Tesla K80. The labeled data is randomly split 90:10, for training and validation.

During each epoch the following augmentations are applied randomly to each image: random crop from 80% to 100% of the image with aspect ratio between 3:4 and 4:3, vertical flip, horizontal flip, and color jitter. The resulting images are then scaled to 3x384x384.

5 FUTURE WORK

Weights for the vision transformer are available for three different model sizes. The base model, large model and huge model each have 12, 24, and 32 transformer blocks, respectively. The pre-trained ViT-Base model was chosen for this experiment, but fine-tuning a larger model will likely result in some improvement. Two of the the publicly available code produced high (top 10% and top 14%) both use EfficientNet B7. Fine tuning on higher resolutions lead to better results.
