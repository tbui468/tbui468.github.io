ABSTRACT

We fine-tune a vision transformer to detect diseases in apples leaf photographs.

1 INTRODUCTION

Food production is becoming more important as the population grows in developing countries. As such, detecting diseases in plants if becoming an important tool in keeping food supplies healthy and readily available. There are not enough experts to inspect all produce, so the burden must be passed onto others. Automatic disease detection using artificial neural networks is one possible solution to this problem. In addition to the costs savings from not requiring opinions of an expert, using an artificial neural network allows detection of diseases in a quick, and accurate manner.

2 RELATED WORK

The Transformer has revolutionized natural language processing (NLP) since being introduced in 2017, analogous to how convolutional networks changed how image processing first by Yan Lecun paper in 1996? and then again by AlexNet in 2012. Although the use of transformers for image classification is still early compared to its use in NLP, early results suggest that transformers perform just as well as convolutional networks at many different tasks. As is common in industry, transfer learning is used to reduce the time needed to train a network for a specific task. One current state-of-the-art convolutional network is Big Transfer (BiT) from Kolesnikov et al. 2020. Transformers are currently not commonly used for vision tasks, but the Vision Transformer (ViT) from Dosovitskiy et al. 2020 suggest that transformers can produce results comparable to modern convolutional networks.

3 METHODS

3.1 DATA

The training data provided consists of 18,632 images and their corresponding labels. The image is labeled by experts with up to the 6 different labels: healthy, frog eye leaf spot, powdery mildew, rust, scab, and complex. The label complex is applied to any specimen with too many diseases to easily classify. In the case that an image is labeled complex, a subset of the diseases may also be labeled. All the jpeg images contain 3 color channels (rgb), and have widths between 2592 and 5184 pixels, and heights between 1728 and 4032 pixels. Reading images multiple large images from disk becomes the main bottleneck during training, therefore all 18632 images were converted to pytorch tensors and saved as a single file on disk. The images are first scaled down so that the smallest spacial dimension is equal to 384, and then centercropped to give a final size of 384x384. The labels are also processed and saved as pytorch tensors.

3.3 MODEL

The ViT weights are publicly available for all three ViT sizes: Base, Large and Huge. All models were pre-trained on ImageNet21k, with variants also further fine-tuned on ImageNet2012. Figure 3 shows the architecture of ViT. The authors of ViT pre-trained the network at a resolution of 224x224, and then fine-tuned at a higher resolution of 384x384. Experimental evidence suggests that fine-tuning at a higher resolution than that of pre-training results in improved performance. We haved followed this procedure and fine-tuned the model on images with an input resolution of 384x384.

Figure 3: Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture. The image was taken directly from "An Image is Worth 16x16 Words: Transformers for Image Recognition at Scale".


4 EXPERIMENT

4.1 SETUP

We used the ViT Base model that was pre-trained on ImageNet21k at 224x224, and then fine-tuned on ImageNet2012 at 384x384. Training was done using Google Cloud Compute, using a NVIDIA Tesla K80. The labeled data is randomly split 90:10, for training and validation. During each epoch the following transformations are applied randomly to each image: random crop from 80% to 100% of the image with aspect ratio between .75 and 1.33, vertical flip, horizontal flip, and color jitter. The resulting images are then scaled to 3x384x384. The 1000 output classifier is replace with a classifier with 6 outputs, one for each label. Only the classifier is trained for the first 10 epochs. The subsequent training epochs include the entire network.

4.2 METRICS

Binary cross entropy loss is used as the loss function, and F-score is used to measure the accuracy.

5 FUTURE WORK

Weights for the vision transformer are available for three different model sizes. The base model, large model and huge model each have 12, 24, and 32 transformer blocks, respectively. The pre-trained ViT-Base model was chosen for this experiment, but fine-tuning a larger model will likely result in some improvement.
